Before you begin · in plain terms

The whole thing in one minute

A bank takes your deposit and lends it out to others. A non-performing loan is money it lent that isn't coming back. The NPL ratio tells you what share of all the bank's loans is like that.

Worked example: a ratio of 41% means that for every 100 taka the banks lent, about 41 never came back. The higher the number, the worse the picture.

01 · The inheritance

Four in ten taka, gone

By 1999, 41.1 percent of everything the banking system had lent was not coming back. This was not one government's doing: it was the sediment of decades, directed lending by nationalised banks since the 1970s, weak appraisal, thin courts, and credit steered by influence under every administration. The Asian crisis and the flood of the century arrived on top of it.

02 · The descent

Twelve years of repair

Then something rare happened: the line fell, and kept falling, across three different governments. Classification rules tightened, recovery courts opened, the central bank was rebuilt, state banks were corporatised. By 2011 the ratio touched 6.1 percent. Proof, worth holding onto, that this problem has been solved here before.

03 · The quiet leak

A decade of flattered numbers

From 2012, scandal followed scandal: Hall-Mark, BASIC Bank, the $81 million cyber heist. Yet the line barely moved. Rescheduling windows, a pandemic moratorium, and two-percent down payments kept troubled loans out of the classified column. The IMF warned in 2019 that the true figure was roughly double what was published.

04 · The rupture

August 2024

Mass protests end fifteen years of one-party rule. An interim government reconstitutes the boards of a dozen banks and orders special audits. Loans of the largest politically connected groups slip into default within months, and the year-end ratio more than doubles to 20.2 percent. The leak becomes a flood.

05 · The reckoning

Highest in the world

In 2025 the 90-day international standard returns, audits bite, and long-hidden distress is booked at once. At 35.7 percent, Bangladesh's ratio passes every reporting country on earth, including economies at war. The sector's capital cushion turns negative for the first time in the country's history.

06 · The open ledger

Recognition is where every recovery began

Rescheduling relief trims the headline to 30.6 percent, but the honest accounting is finally on the table: distressed assets near six taka in ten. India, Indonesia, and Nepal each stood near this ledge, and each climbed down, starting exactly here. The full chart, every annotation, and those comparisons follow below.

◍ / YOU

What this means for youএর মানে আপনার জন্য কী

You may never take out a bank loan. It still reaches you, through your deposit, your taxes, and the price of everything you buy. Three questions, answered plainly.

Is my money in the bank safe?

A bank absorbs losses with its own capital cushion (CRAR) before anything touches your deposit. For the sector as a whole, that cushion has now fallen below zero for the first time in the country's history, the airbag has deflated. Your deposit is still backed by the central bank and deposit insurance, but the system has almost no room left for another shock.

Who actually pays to fix it?

When a state bank runs out of capital, the government refills it with public money, your taxes. Every taka spent rescuing a bank is a taka not spent on a school, a hospital, or a road. The unpaid loans of a powerful few quietly become a bill sent to everyone.

Why care if I have no loan?

Wounded banks lend less and charge more, which slows jobs and business. To plug the holes, more money tends to get printed, and that shows up as inflation, a weaker taka, and costlier imports, from rice to fuel. A banking crisis is a cost-of-living story wearing a suit.

The neighbourhood, and the mirrorতুলনা

Two comparisons place the line in the world. First, a snapshot of late 2025, when Bangladesh's ratio exceeded economies at war or in sovereign default. Then five trajectories, neighbours and structurally similar economies, each of which faced its own bad-loan mountain and climbed down. The paths differed; the first step never did: recognising the losses in full.

Think of it like this

Every country here once climbed its own bad-loan mountain, some with a forced audit like India, some with an asset-management company like Indonesia. Whatever the route, the first step down was always the same: honestly recognising the losses. Bangladesh has just taken that first step.

Reading the line honestly

A time series this long is not measured with one ruler. The definition of a non-performing loan in Bangladesh changed at least three times across these decades, and each change moves the line for reasons unrelated to borrower behaviour in that year.

The 2012 jump partly reflects stricter, internationally aligned classification rules. The flat 2019 to 2023 stretch reflects the opposite force: rescheduling schemes, a pandemic moratorium, and relaxed down-payment rules that kept troubled loans out of the classified column. This is where the overlay layers earn their place. The IMF flagged as early as 2019 that true distressed assets were roughly double the reported figure, and the central bank's own distressed-asset measure reached 59 percent of all credit by end 2025. The capital layer shows the consequence: once hidden losses were recognised, the sector's cushion fell from 11.6 percent to negative territory in under two years, a collapse with almost no peacetime precedent.

Read together, the layers separate two stories the headline blends: how credit quality actually evolved, and how much of the truth each era's measurement regime allowed to be seen.

Glossary · in plain terms

  • Non-performing loan (NPL) A loan that isn't coming back, usually more than 90 days overdue, or otherwise unlikely to be repaid. Example: lending 100 taka and not getting 41 of it back.
  • Capital adequacy ratio (CRAR) How much of its own capital a bank holds against its risks, the cushion for absorbing losses. Basel III sets a 10% floor; below zero means the cushion is exhausted.
  • Classified loans The loans officials formally mark as non-performing under the rules. When the rules change, this number changes too.
  • Rescheduling Relaxing a loan's timeline or terms so it counts as "current" again. It lowers the reported NPL ratio on paper, not the real risk.
  • Distressed assets Defaulted + rescheduled + written-off loans together, a broader measure of the real problem, often far larger than reported NPLs.
  • Provisioning Money a bank sets aside against expected losses. Setting aside too little makes profit look bigger but hides the risk.

Method and sources

  • NPL series. Gross classified loans as a share of total outstanding loans, all scheduled banks, year-end. 1997–2011 from Bangladesh Bank annual reports via published academic compilations; 2012–2025 from Bangladesh Bank quarterly statistics, cross-checked against World Bank indicators and CEIC.
  • CRAR series. Sector Capital to Risk-Weighted Assets Ratio from Bangladesh Bank Financial Stability Reports and quarterly assessments; plotted from 2020 with quarterly points through the 2024–25 decline. Basel III reference at the 10 percent regulatory minimum.
  • Distressed estimates. IMF staff estimate of actual distressed loans (2019, roughly double the reported stock); Bangladesh Bank Financial Stability Report distressed-asset measures for 2024 and 2025 combining defaulted, rescheduled, and written-off loans.
  • Sep 2025 marker. The annual series uses December values; the 35.7 percent quarterly peak is shown separately.
  • Cross-country data. Late-2025 snapshot from national regulators as compiled by the ADB NPL Watch and press analyses. Trajectory panels use anchor years from the RBI, SBP, CBSL, Bank Indonesia and OJK, NRB, and the World Bank NPL indicator; values between anchors are linearly connected and definitions vary across regimes, so panels are best read for shape rather than decimal precision.
  • Neutrality. Era shading marks who held office; it implies no causal claim. Where responsibility is contested, entries state what occurred and what the records show.
